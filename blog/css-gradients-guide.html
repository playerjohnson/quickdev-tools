CSS Gradients: A Visual Guide with Copy-Paste Examples

14 February 2026 · 7 min read

Linear Gradients

Linear gradients create a smooth transition between colours along a straight line. The simplest form needs just two colours — the browser handles the rest.

background: linear-gradient(to right, #667eea, #764ba2);

You can control the direction with angles or keywords:

  • to right (or 90deg) — left to right
  • to bottom (or 180deg) — top to bottom (default)
  • to bottom right (or 135deg) — diagonal
  • 45deg — custom angle, measured clockwise from top

Multiple colour stops:

background: linear-gradient(135deg, #f093fb 0%, #f5576c 50%, #4facfe 100%);

Radial Gradients

Radial gradients radiate from a centre point outward. Great for spotlight effects, glowing buttons and vignettes.

background: radial-gradient(circle, #ff9a9e, #fad0c4);

Control the shape (circle vs ellipse) and position:

background: radial-gradient(ellipse at top left, #a18cd1, #fbc2eb);

Conic Gradients

Conic gradients rotate colour transitions around a centre point — perfect for pie charts, colour wheels and decorative effects.

background: conic-gradient(#ff6b6b, #feca57, #48dbfb, #ff9ff3, #ff6b6b);

Gradient Performance Tips

  • CSS gradients are rendered by the GPU — they perform better than gradient images
  • Avoid animating gradient colours directly — use opacity or transform tricks instead
  • Use background-size for repeating patterns — smaller gradients tiled with background-repeat are efficient
  • Combine gradients with background-blend-mode for sophisticated effects without extra elements
